SECTION 3: ADVANCED PROMPTING TECHNIQUE — ROLE-PLAYING + FEW-SHOT
================================================================================

Strategy: Combine TWO techniques for maximum quality:
  1. Role-Playing — assign Claude the persona of a "Senior Business Communication Expert"
  2. Few-Shot Examples — provide 1-2 worked examples in the prompt itself
  3. Chain-of-Thought — instruct the model to reason before writing

Why this works: Role-playing sets quality baseline, few-shot anchors format+tone,
and CoT ensures facts are not missed.

--- PROMPT TEMPLATE (paste in generator.py) ---

SYSTEM_PROMPT = """
You are a Senior Business Communication Expert with 15 years of experience writing 
professional emails across industries including finance, tech, consulting, and HR.
You are known for:
- Perfect tone calibration (formal to casual, urgent to empathetic)
- Seamlessly weaving in key facts without sounding like a list
- Writing emails that are concise yet complete — never padded, never missing anything
- Strong, purposeful subject lines and clear calls to action

Your task: Generate a professional email given an intent, a set of key facts, and a 
desired tone.

ALWAYS follow this reasoning process before writing:
1. THINK: Identify the core goal of this email. What action or response does it seek?
2. PLAN: Decide the optimal structure (greeting → context → facts → ask → close).
3. WRITE: Draft the email, ensuring every key fact is included naturally.
4. CHECK: Confirm the tone matches exactly, all facts are present, and the email 
   is polished.

Return ONLY the final email. No preamble, no explanation, no markdown fences.
The output must be a ready-to-send email with Subject line, body, and sign-off.
"""

FEW_SHOT_EXAMPLE = """
--- EXAMPLE ---
INTENT: Follow up after a product demo to a potential client
KEY FACTS:
- Demo happened on June 5th
- Client showed interest in the analytics dashboard feature
- Pricing starts at $299/month with a 14-day free trial available
- Next step: schedule a 30-minute Q&A call
TONE: Professional but warm

OUTPUT:
Subject: Following Up on Yesterday's Demo — Next Steps

Hi [Client Name],

Thank you for taking the time to join us for the demo on June 5th. It was great 
walking you through the platform, and I noticed your interest in the analytics 
dashboard — it really is one of our most powerful features.

I wanted to follow up and share that our plans start at $299/month, and we do offer 
a 14-day free trial so you can explore the full feature set at no commitment.

I'd love to set up a quick 30-minute Q&A call at your convenience to answer any 
remaining questions. Would sometime next week work for you?

Looking forward to hearing from you.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
--- END EXAMPLE ---
"""

SECTION 6: src/evaluator.py — 3 CUSTOM METRICS
================================================================================

--- METRIC 1: FACT RECALL SCORE ---
Definition: What percentage of the required key facts are present in the generated email?
Logic:
  - For each fact in the scenario's facts list, check if the core information
    is semantically present in the generated email.
  - Use sentence-transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) to compute cosine similarity
    between each fact and the email text.
  - A fact is "recalled" if max similarity across all sentences > threshold (0.45).
  - Score = (facts recalled / total facts) * 100
Range: 0–100

Code outline:
  from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer, util
  model = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2')
  
  def fact_recall_score(generated_email: str, facts: list[str]) -> float:
      email_sentences = split_into_sentences(generated_email)
      email_embeddings = model.encode(email_sentences)
      recalled = 0
      for fact in facts:
          fact_embedding = model.encode(fact)
          similarities = util.cos_sim(fact_embedding, email_embeddings)
          if float(similarities.max()) > 0.45:
              recalled += 1
      return round((recalled / len(facts)) * 100, 2)

--- METRIC 2: TONE ALIGNMENT SCORE ---
Definition: How well does the generated email's tone match the requested tone?
Logic:
  - Use an LLM-as-a-Judge approach.
  - Send the generated email + the requested tone to Claude/GPT-4o-mini.
  - Ask it to score tone alignment from 1–10 with a brief reason.
  - Parse the numeric score from the response.
  - Normalize to 0–100.
Range: 0–100

Prompt for judge:
  "You are a tone analysis expert. Given the requested tone and the email below,
   rate how well the email achieves the requested tone on a scale of 1 to 10.
   
   Requested Tone: {tone}
   Email: {email}
   
   Respond in this exact format:
   SCORE: [number]
   REASON: [one sentence]"

Code outline:
  def tone_alignment_score(generated_email: str, requested_tone: str) -> float:
      judge_prompt = build_judge_prompt(generated_email, requested_tone)
      response = call_judge_llm(judge_prompt)  # Use gpt-4o-mini or claude-haiku
      score = parse_score_from_response(response)  # regex: r'SCORE:\s*(\d+)'
      return round((score / 10) * 100, 2)

--- METRIC 3: FLUENCY & CONCISENESS SCORE ---
Definition: Is the email well-written, grammatically clean, and appropriately concise?
Logic:
  - Two sub-components averaged together:
  
  A) Fluency (50% weight):
     - Use textstat.flesch_reading_ease(email) to get readability score.
     - Professional emails should score 50–70 (standard readable).
     - Normalize: score = min(100, max(0, flesch_score)) for simplicity.
     - Penalize very low (<30) or very high (>80 = too simple) scores.
  
  B) Conciseness (50% weight):
     - Count word count of generated email vs human reference email.
     - Ideal range: 0.7x to 1.3x the reference length.
     - Score = 100 if in range, else decay proportionally.
     - Formula: conciseness = max(0, 100 - abs(ratio - 1.0) * 150)
     - where ratio = len(generated.split()) / len(reference.split())
  
  Final score = (fluency_score + conciseness_score) / 2
Range: 0–100

Code outline:
  import textstat
  
  def fluency_conciseness_score(generated_email: str, human_reference: str) -> float:
      flesch = textstat.flesch_reading_ease(generated_email)
      fluency = min(100, max(0, flesch + 30))  # shift to penalize very hard text
      
      gen_words = len(generated_email.split())
      ref_words = len(human_reference.split())
      ratio = gen_words / ref_words if ref_words > 0 else 1.0
      conciseness = max(0, 100 - abs(ratio - 1.0) * 150)
      
      return round((fluency + conciseness) / 2, 2)

--- COMPOSITE SCORE ---
  def composite_score(fact_recall, tone_alignment, fluency_conciseness) -> float:
      # Weighted: facts are most critical, then tone, then fluency
      return round(
          (fact_recall * 0.40) + (tone_alignment * 0.35) + (fluency_conciseness * 0.25),
          2
      )

SECTION 14: TIPS TO ACE THE ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

1. WRITE GOOD HUMAN REFERENCES
   Your 10 human reference emails are your benchmark. Make them genuinely good —
   correct tone, all facts embedded naturally, professional sign-offs. These
   directly affect your metric scores.

2. TUNE THE FACT RECALL THRESHOLD
   Start with cosine similarity threshold 0.45. If you find false positives/negatives
   in testing, adjust between 0.35–0.55. Print similarity scores during dev.

3. USE A CHEAP MODEL AS JUDGE
   For Tone Alignment (LLM-as-a-Judge), use claude-haiku or gpt-4o-mini to keep
   API costs low when running all 10 scenarios × 2 models = 20 judgments.

4. SHOW YOUR WORK IN THE REPORT
   Interviewers want to see metric definitions, not just scores. Be explicit:
   "Metric 1 uses semantic similarity with threshold 0.45 because..."

5. VARIANCE MATTERS
   When recommending a model, mention not just average scores but consistency.
   A model with avg 85 but low variance beats avg 87 with high variance for prod.

6. MAKE THE CSV CLEAN
   Your CSV output is a deliverable. Round scores to 2 decimal places,
   use clear column names, include scenario intent in the CSV for readability.

7. DOCUMENT YOUR PROMPT TEMPLATE FULLY
   The system prompt, few-shot example, and user template should all appear
   verbatim in your final report PDF. This is explicitly asked for.
